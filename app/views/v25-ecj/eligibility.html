Check if you’re eligible

You may be able to get Healthy Start payments if you:

  • live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland
  • are more than 10 weeks pregnant or have at least one child under the age of 4
  • get certain benefits
  • are the main carer for a child – we cannot make Healthy Start payments to more than one parent or carer for the same child
What is a main carer?

The main carer for a child is the parent or carer they live with most of the time. This is the carer who should apply for Healthy Start payments.

You’ll need to decide which one parent or carer will apply for Healthy Start if you:

  • do not live with your child’s other parent or carer and you have equal child arrangements (opens in a new tab) - this also known as 50-50 custody
  • live together with your child and their other parent or carer in the same household If your child’s other parent or carer gets Universal Credit or another qualifying benefit, you should check that they do not already get Healthy Start payments before you apply.

If your child’s other parent or carer gets Universal Credit or another qualifying benefit, you should check that they do not already get Healthy Start payments before you apply.

Information:
If you’re under 18

You can get Healthy Start payments once you’re more than 10 weeks pregnant, even if you or your family do not get any benefits.

Benefits you need to get

You can get Healthy Start payments for your pregnancy or children under 4 if you get:

  • Universal Credit – your monthly take-home pay must be £408 or less
  • Child Tax Credit – your annual household income must be £16,190 or less
  • Income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance (JSA)
  • Income Support
  • Pension Credit including the child addition

If you get Income-related Employment and Support Allowance (ESA), you can get Healthy Start payments from your tenth week of pregnancy until your baby is born.

If you are not a British citizen but your child is

You can apply for Healthy Start if all of the following statements are true.

  1. Your child is a British citizen – you can check if they’re a British citizen (opens in a new tab).
  2. Your family earns £408 or less per month after tax.
  3. Your biometric residence permit (BRP) or online immigration status (opens in a new tab) say you cannot claim public funds such as benefits (opens in a new tab)– you might also have a letter from the Home Office about this.
